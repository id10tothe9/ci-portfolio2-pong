The game Pong as we know it today was brought to life by Atari's co-founder Nolan Bushnell, who hired Allan Alcorn because of his experience with electrical engineering and computer science and asked him to create the game as a warm up exercise.

Bushnell was inspired after playing the ping-pong game demonstration of Magnavox Odyssey, the first ever commercial home video game console. In 2011, Bushnell stated that the game was inspired by a previous version of electronic tennis he had played on a PDP-1 computer in 1964 while attending college.

Alcorn built Pong using transistor-transistor logic (TTL), an early form of digital logic design that was popular before the advent of integrated circuits. He improved on the original idea creating a game that was so good that Bushnell decided to market it as the first game of Atari in 1972, soon after the company's establishment.

Due to its significant cultural impact, Pong is now part of the permanent collection at the Smithsonian Institution in Washington, D.C.

You are engaged in a game of Pong against the computer. Each player controls a vertical paddle on the screen. Your objective: be the first to reach eleven points. Score when your opponent fails to return the ball.

The ball bounces in straight lines off the paddles and the top and bottom of the screen. The angle depends on where it hits your paddle.

Pong is not only about scoring points but also about outlasting your opponent. As the game progresses, the ball speeds up, making it an intense battle of reaction and endurance!

Interestingly, in the original design, the paddles couldn't reach the screen's top due to a circuit defect. Instead of fixing the issue, Allan Alcorn decided to keep it, adding a unique challenge to the game.
